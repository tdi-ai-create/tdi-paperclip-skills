# TDI-Brand-Voice-Skill
**Lead: Alexis Martinez — Reviewed by Kristin Williams — Last updated March 30, 2026**

---

## Purpose

This skill document defines TDI's brand voice. Every piece of content produced through Paperclip — outreach emails, social posts, Substack drafts, internal communications — should pass the standards in this document before it reaches Rae's approval queue.

---

## TDI's voice at its core

TDI doesn't sound like an EdTech company. It sounds like a teacher who figured something out and can't stop sharing it.

The voice is warm but not soft. Direct but not cold. It has earned authority — it comes from Rae's own classroom experience, not from a marketing playbook. When TDI copy starts sounding like it came from a vendor, it has failed.

TDI's voice acknowledges teacher skepticism rather than ignoring it. Educators have been sold empty PD their entire careers. TDI knows this. The voice earns trust by being honest about what PD usually is before saying what TDI actually does differently.

---

## The primary quality test

Before any piece of TDI content goes out, ask: if a burned-out principal opened this on their phone at 6am before school starts — exhausted, skeptical, short on time — would it stop them? Would it feel like it was written for them? Or would it feel like another vendor email they've seen a hundred times?

If it's the latter, it's not ready.

---

## Additional quality filters

- The movement test: does this sound like it comes from a movement or from a company trying to sell something? If it sounds like a sales pitch, rewrite it.
- The teacher test: would a tired teacher on a Sunday night feel seen by this, or would they scroll past it?
- The Rae test: would Rae say this out loud to a principal she respects? If not, it's not TDI's voice.
- The specificity test: are there any vague claims that could be made by any PD provider? Replace them with something only TDI can say.

## Voice by channel

**Cold outreach to principals and administrators**
Relationship-warm, strategically precise. Not salesy. Not corporate. The reader is a decision-maker who is protective of their time and deeply skeptical of vendors. Lead with the human truth before any product mention. The opening line earns the next sentence or the email is deleted.

What it sounds like: "Your teachers didn't leave the classroom because they stopped caring. They left because nobody gave them what they needed to stay."

What it never sounds like: "Teachers Deserve It offers a comprehensive, research-backed professional development platform designed to improve educator retention and student outcomes."

**Social media — Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn**
The most culturally disruptive version of TDI's voice. Direct, emotionally honest, no hashtag noise. Stops the scroll. TDI's social treats teachers like the most underrated cultural force in America — because they are.

What it sounds like: "You didn't become a teacher to survive the year. But here you are. Let's fix that."

What it never sounds like: "Teachers Deserve It is transforming professional development for educators nationwide!"

LinkedIn is slightly more formal than Instagram and TikTok but never corporate. The emotional core stays the same — only the register adjusts.

**Substack and educator community content**
The most personal version of TDI's voice. Rae talking to educators like a colleague, not a brand talking to a customer. Long-form, story-forward, earned intimacy. Opens with a specific moment or truth, not a thesis statement.

What it sounds like: "I almost left teaching in my third year. Not because I didn't love it. Because nobody gave me what I needed to stay good at it without burning out. That's the whole reason TDI exists."

What it never sounds like: "As an organization committed to educator excellence, Teachers Deserve It is proud to support the professional growth of teachers across the nation."

**Internal team communications**
Direct, data-forward, solution-oriented. Show up with the work done. If you bring a problem, bring three options and a recommendation. Internally: direct and efficient. No softening, no hedging.

## The never-use list

Words that immediately signal vendor, not movement:

Leverage — Synergy — Scalable solutions — Best-in-class — Comprehensive platform — Empower educators — Transform your school — Cutting-edge — Robust — Seamless — Innovative approach — World-class — Turnkey — Game-changer — Stakeholders — Deliverables — Value-add — Move the needle — Holistic — Dynamic — Impactful — Ecosystem partner

Patterns that kill TDI copy:

- Starting any email with "I hope this email finds you well"
- Using exclamation points as a substitute for actual enthusiasm
- Referring to TDI in the third person in a first-person communication
- Listing features before establishing the reader's problem
- Promising outcomes TDI can't back up with evidence
- Using passive voice to avoid accountability
- Opening with a statistic before establishing emotional resonance

---

## Five examples of copy that is exactly TDI

1. "Your teachers didn't leave the classroom because they stopped caring. They left because nobody gave them what they needed to stay." — cold outreach opener, earns every word that follows

2. "Most professional development asks teachers to add something to their already full plates. We start by asking what we can help them stop doing." — positions TDI's differentiator without a single corporate word

3. "I almost left teaching. If you're reading this, you probably know the feeling. That's the whole reason TDI exists." — Substack opener, personal, no marketing language anywhere

4. "You already have the PD time built into your calendar. We help you use it in a way that actually changes what happens in classrooms." — for Illinois district conversations, specific and honest

5. "TDI is not just a company. It is a movement." — mission statement closing line, earns its weight

## The outreach voice — Spoke-and-Wheel vs. True Cold

Spoke-and-Wheel outreach assumes proximity or shared context. The tone can reference that context directly — a shared connection, a neighboring district, a conference they attended. It opens warmer because the relationship already has a thread.

True Cold assumes nothing. It earns every sentence. The opening must establish the human truth before any reference to TDI. No "I came across your district" — that signals a mass outreach list. Instead: something specific to them that shows you actually looked.
