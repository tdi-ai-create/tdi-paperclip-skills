## What TDI is — in grant language

Teachers Deserve It is a professional development organization that delivers the TDI Blueprint — a three-phase school transformation model (Ignite, Accelerate, Sustain) — to K-12 schools across 21+ states. TDI serves the full education ecosystem: classroom teachers, paraprofessionals, support staff, and extracurricular educators.

TDI's work is research-backed, educator-tried, and implementation-focused. It is not a one-day workshop. It is a sustained, school-embedded partnership that builds educator capacity over time and tracks outcomes through data.

In grant language: TDI is an evidence-based professional learning provider that delivers measurable outcomes in teacher sustainability, administrator effectiveness, and student achievement.

## What makes TDI fundable

- Sustained model, not one-off PD — reviewers consistently fund multi-phase, implementation-focused approaches over single workshops
- Serves paraprofessionals and support staff — an underserved population most PD providers ignore, which differentiates TDI in competitive applications
- Data-driven outcomes — the Implementation and Compliance Analytics dashboard provides measurable proof points reviewers require
- Broad ecosystem reach — serving teachers, paras, support staff, and extracurricular educators expands eligible use cases across multiple grant categories
- 87,000+ educators across 21+ states — demonstrates scale and proven demand
- RatedEd rating of 8.9/10 — third-party validation that strengthens evidence base claims

## The five federal grant categories TDI maps to

**Title II, Part A** — Professional development for teachers and school leaders. TDI's primary federal lane. Defensible because TDI's work is sustained, research-backed, and tied to a school improvement plan. Key language: "evidence-based professional learning," "job-embedded coaching," "teacher retention and sustainability."

**IDEA Part B (611/619)** — Special education professional development. Para and SpEd team training is a direct match. Key language: "paraprofessional capacity building," "IEP implementation support," "inclusive classroom practices."

**Title I, Part A** — Whole-school improvement for high-poverty schools. TDI's Blueprint aligns to school improvement plan goals. Key language: "comprehensive school improvement," "evidence-based intervention," "educator effectiveness in high-need schools."

**Title III, Part A** — English Language Learner support. Bilingual para training and ELL educator PD. Key language: "bilingual paraprofessional development," "English learner instructional support," "culturally responsive professional learning."

**Title IV, Part A (SSAE)** — Well-rounded educational opportunities. SEL, PBIS, educator wellness. Key language: "educator sustainability," "school climate and culture," "social-emotional learning professional development."

## The board-ready funding justification

When a district administrator needs to justify TDI spending to a school board, they use this language:

"Teachers Deserve It delivers a multi-phase, research-backed professional development partnership that directly supports our school improvement goals. Unlike one-time PD events, the TDI Blueprint builds sustained educator capacity through embedded coaching and data tracking. Federal funds allow us to invest in the kind of professional learning that actually changes classroom practice — and the data shows it."

This paragraph is the most-used piece of language TDI produces for district partners. Every grant application should include a version of it.

## Words and phrases that kill applications — never use these

- Transformational (overused, unverifiable)
- World-class (marketing language)
- Cutting-edge (signals vendor, not practitioner)
- Comprehensive platform (EdTech vendor language)
- Empower educators (vague, every PD provider says this)
- Scalable solution (corporate language)
- Best-in-class (meaningless to reviewers)
- Innovative (unless followed by specific evidence of innovation)
- Stakeholders (bureaucratic — use "teachers," "administrators," "families")
- Deliverables (use "outcomes" or "results" instead)

---

## The three most common reasons strong EdTech applications get rejected

1. Features before problems — leading with what the product does before establishing the problem the funder cares about. Always open with the pain, then the solution.
2. No implementation plan — reviewers want to know how the PD actually gets delivered, not just what it covers. TDI's Blueprint phases (Ignite, Accelerate, Sustain) are the answer to this.
3. Vague outcome claims — "improved teacher morale" is not a measurable outcome. "Reduced educator attrition by X%" is. Always attach TDI's outcome claims to measurable data where possible.

## Illinois-specific positioning

Illinois districts have built-in PD time (TI Days, SIP days) and a waiver process that can double available PD time. TDI's message for Illinois: "You already have the time. We help you use it well."

Illinois Regional Offices of Education (ROEs) are a key entry point for grant-funded PD. SIP day approval through ROEs is a funding pathway worth naming in applications targeting Illinois districts.
